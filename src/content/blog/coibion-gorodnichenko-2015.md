Imagine you are an economist.

Tragic, yes. But stay with it.

You are trying to model the economy. Households, firms, investors, workers, forecasters, and central bankers need to make decisions about the future. Inflation. Output. Interest rates. Wages. Growth. The usual list of things people predict right before being ambushed by the next data release.

Now, you have a choice.

You could model people as they are: distracted, busy, under-informed, institutionally constrained, occasionally clever, and usually updating their beliefs only after reality has become too embarrassing to ignore.

Or you could assume they process all available information instantly and correctly.

Naturally, economics chose the second option.

This is full-information rational expectations, or FIRE, which sounds less like an economic assumption and more like something shouted during an evacuation. In this world, people observe all relevant information and make forecasts wrong only because the future contains genuine surprises.

Very elegant. Also completely unhinged.

Because the real world does not run on instant belief updates. People do not process macroeconomic data like servers receiving a software patch. They update like committees. Slowly. Defensively. With revisions, caveats, and someone saying, "Let's revisit this after the next inflation print."

This is where Olivier Coibion and Yuriy Gorodnichenko's 2015 paper, "Information Rigidity and the Expectations Formation Process," becomes useful. The paper asks a beautifully annoying question: when forecasts are wrong, are they random, or do they reveal people are systematically behind the curve?

And that was the problem. Once you ask that properly, the rational-expectations machine starts making a worrying noise.

## The Paper in One Paragraph

Coibion and Gorodnichenko propose a simple test for information rigidity.

Instead of asking whether forecasts are wrong, which is about as useful as discovering politicians enjoy power, they ask whether forecast errors are predictable from previous forecast revisions.

Under full-information rational expectations, they should not be. If forecasters used all available information, today's revision should not systematically predict tomorrow's mistake.

But if forecasters update slowly, partially, or through noisy signals, then forecast revisions should predict future forecast errors.

In plain English: people change their minds, but not enough.

That is the core of the paper. Not that forecasters are stupid. That would be too easy. The deeper point is that even rational people update slowly when information is costly, noisy, delayed, or difficult to interpret.

Very human. Very inconvenient.

## The Fantasy: Everyone Knows Everything

Rational expectations is useful because it gives economists a clean benchmark.

The claim is not that people can predict the future perfectly. That would be insane. The claim is that they use available information efficiently. Their forecasts may be wrong, but their errors should not be systematically predictable.

Forecast errors are allowed. Predictable forecast errors are the scandal.

Under full-information rational expectations, if inflation turns out higher than expected, fine. A shock happened. Oil prices jumped. Supply chains collapsed. These things happen.

But what should not happen is this: forecasters revise inflation expectations upwards, then still systematically underpredict inflation afterwards.

That would mean the revision itself contained information they failed to fully incorporate. Small issue.

That is exactly what Coibion and Gorodnichenko investigate. They take the vague suspicion that people are slow to update and put it in a table, which makes it respectable, even if the table is accusing everyone of being late.

This matters because expectations are not decorative. They affect wage-setting, price-setting, hiring, investment, borrowing, and monetary policy. If expectations update instantly, policy works through one economy. If they update slowly, policy works through fog. And fog matters.

## The Real Human: Slow, Noisy, Distracted

The paper focuses on two broad models of imperfect information.

The first is sticky information. In sticky-information models, people update occasionally. When they update, they may form rational expectations. Between updates, they are basically running on old software.

This is extremely believable. You form a view. Then life happens. Rent. Deadlines. Emails. Suddenly, the economy has moved on and you are still forecasting from three data releases ago.

The second is noisy information. Here, people update more continuously, but the signals are imperfect. They infer the economy through revisions, measurement error, media noise, and central bank communication so polished it somehow means three incompatible things at once.

They update gradually. Not because they are irrational, but because the signal is messy.

The paper is not saying, "Forecasters are idiots." It is saying something more interesting: forecasters may be rational given what they know, but what they know is incomplete, delayed, noisy, and processed through institutions that still think Outlook meeting invites are civilisation.

## The Test: Forecasts Betray the Forecaster

The genius of the paper is that the test is simple.

Take the forecast error. Ask whether it can be predicted by the previous forecast revision.

In simplified form: Forecast Error = c + beta times Forecast Revision + error.

The interpretation is the whole game.

If beta equals 0, forecast revisions do not predict future forecast errors. That is what full-information rational expectations suggests. Forecasters revised their beliefs, used the available information, and any remaining error is just the universe being rude.

But if beta is greater than 0, forecasters are underreacting. They revise in the right direction, but not enough.

The economy says: "Inflation is rising." Forecasters say: "Fine, we will revise inflation expectations upwards." The economy says: "No, more." Forecasters say: "Let's not be dramatic." Six months later, everyone is staring at the data like it personally betrayed them.

The elegant part is that beta is not just a random coefficient sitting in a table trying to look employable. In the models studied, it maps into the underlying degree of information rigidity. So the regression estimates how slowly expectations adjust.

That is why the paper matters. It turns "people are behind the curve" from a pub-level complaint into an empirical object.

## Professional Forecasters Underreact

Coibion and Gorodnichenko apply this framework to professional forecasts, including US inflation forecasts.

This is where it gets fun.

For US professional inflation forecasts from 1969 to 2010, the paper estimates this coefficient at about 1.23. In the sticky-information interpretation, this implies an information rigidity parameter of around 0.55, meaning agents update roughly every six to seven months on average. Under the imperfect-information interpretation, it implies that agents put less than half their weight on new information.

Pause there. Six to seven months. For professional forecasters. Not your uncle explaining monetary policy through petrol prices and vibes. Professional forecasters. Spreadsheet clergy.

The finding is not that forecasters never update. They do. They revise. They respond to data. They are just not turning the wheel enough.

Underreaction is not ignorance. It is inertia. And inertia is often more powerful because it looks respectable. Ignorance says, "I have no idea." Inertia says, "Our baseline scenario remains broadly intact."

Very professional. Very wrong.

The forecast revision says: "We saw the new information." The forecast error says: "We did not believe it enough."

One objection is that professional forecasters may smooth their forecasts for reputational reasons. Nobody wants to look erratic. If you change your view too aggressively, clients may assume you are guessing. Which, to be fair, you are.

The paper takes this seriously. They compare professional forecasts with consumer expectations from the Michigan Survey and market-based expectations. They find qualitatively similar evidence of information rigidity across these groups.

So this is not just office politics. Beliefs diffuse slowly because attention is scarce, information is noisy, and changing your mind is costly.

A revision is not a conversion. It is a negotiation.

## The World Is Not Just Sticky. It Is Noisy.

At this point, it would be convenient if the paper simply declared one model the winner. Sticky information defeats noisy information. Everyone applauds. The losing model leaves quietly and gets a job in consulting.

But economics is rarely that merciful.

Both models can generate the same basic prediction: forecast errors are predictable from forecast revisions.

Sticky information suggests people update their whole information set only occasionally. If that is the whole story, rigidity should look similar across variables and horizons.

Noisy information is more flexible. Some variables are easier to observe. Some are more persistent. Some have cleaner signals. Some are statistical compost with a press release attached.

Using broader macroeconomic forecasts and cross-country data, the authors show that information rigidity varies across variables and countries in ways consistent with imperfect-information models. Persistence and signal-to-noise measures explain a meaningful share of the variation.

In simpler terms: people are not just late. They are squinting.

If the world is noisy, underreaction is not simply a behavioural defect. If every signal might be wrong, revised, or temporary, you do not jump fully every time the data twitches. You move gradually.

That sounds sensible. Until the signal was real all along.

## Stability Breeds Inattention

This is the most interesting part of the paper. And the most unsettling.

Coibion and Gorodnichenko examine how information rigidity changes over time. In the US, they find that information rigidity fell during the volatile 1970s and early 1980s, reaching a low around 1983 to 1984, before rising again during the Great Moderation, when macroeconomic volatility became more subdued.

This is deliciously bleak.

When the economy is unstable, people pay attention. When the economy becomes stable, people stop paying attention. Then the economy becomes easier to misunderstand when the next shock arrives.

Naturally, this is considered progress.

The Great Moderation is often described as better monetary policy, structural change, lower volatility, and the administrative miracle of making capitalism appear less drunk. And to be fair, people enjoy not living inside a macroeconomic washing machine.

But stability has a side effect. It teaches people that vigilance is unnecessary.

If inflation stays low for long enough, it stops feeling like a live risk. If central banks appear competent for long enough, people begin to mistake competence for omnipotence.

Cute. But no.

The paper's interpretation is that stabilisation can promote inattention. When volatility falls, the incentive to acquire and process information falls with it. Why spend scarce attention monitoring a variable that barely moves?

This is rational. And that is what makes it worrying.

The danger is not stupidity. The danger is reasonable inattention in a temporarily calm system. It is the financial equivalent of not checking the fire alarm because the building has not burned down recently.

## Big Shocks Wake Everyone Up

The same logic also works in reverse.

The paper finds evidence of state-dependence. Information rigidity declines during recessions after a few quarters, then gradually recovers. The authors also study 9/11 and find that professional forecasts were not subject to the same important information rigidities shortly afterwards.

Nothing improves attention quite like the building being on fire.

Attention is allocated. When conditions are boring, people economise on attention. When conditions become dramatic, they suddenly discover the data tab.

Expectations are not formed through a fixed mechanical rule. They depend on the state of the world.

Calm produces one kind of belief formation. Crisis produces another. During normal times, bad news may diffuse slowly. During crises, everyone updates at once.

Which is, of course, how you get panic.

## Central Banks and the Theatre of Anchored Expectations

Central banks love the phrase anchored expectations.

It sounds reassuring. Nautical. Responsible. Like inflation has been tied to a dock by serious adults in sensible shoes.

But "anchored" can mean several things. It can mean expectations are stable because policy is credible, because nobody is paying attention, or because the shock has not arrived yet.

Macroeconomics, being civilised, handles this by giving it a name and moving on.

Coibion and Gorodnichenko find that central bank independence is associated with greater information rigidity, while inflation targeting appears to have little effect among professional forecasters in their sample.

That does not mean inflation targeting is useless. Do not be that person.

The point is narrower. A credible regime may reduce volatility, and reduced volatility may reduce attention. Reduced attention may make expectations look anchored until they are not.

Very convenient. Until the anchor turns out to be made of vibes.

## Why This Matters for Finance

The finance angle is obvious.

Markets are often described as fast, forward-looking, and brutally efficient. Which is true, except when it is not, which is often enough to keep finance employed.

If agents update slowly, prices may underreact to news. If major shocks wake everyone up at once, prices may move violently. If stable regimes breed complacency, risk premia may compress precisely when attention should be rising.

Because of course they can.

This connects naturally to inflation surprises, rate expectations, momentum, macro trading, post-earnings drift, and markets pretending to be calm until they are suddenly not.

Markets are not a single omniscient brain. They are a crowd of models, mandates, incentives, risk limits, career concerns, and people pretending they are not emotionally involved.

The Coibion-Gorodnichenko framework gives a clean diagnostic: do not just ask whether expectations are wrong. Ask whether the errors reveal underreaction.

Less glamorous than "finding alpha", obviously, but more useful than pretending the market is a divine calculator with a Bloomberg subscription.

## What I Would Be Careful About

The paper is strong, but it should not be abused.

First, many results use average forecasts. Aggregation can hide a lot. One forecaster may update aggressively, another slowly, and the average may look like a neat process no individual follows. Averages are useful. They are also where nuance dies.

Second, professional forecasters are not households, firms, traders, or wage negotiators. If even they show rigidity, that is important, but it does not automatically tell us exactly how everyone else updates.

Third, forecast revisions predicting forecast errors may reflect information rigidities, but interpretation still requires care. One regression does not solve society. Annoying, but true.

Fourth, macroeconomic data itself is messy. Realisations are revised. Variables are noisy. The data are not tablets brought down from the mountain. They are estimates wearing a tie.

Still, the central insight survives: predictable forecast errors are evidence that the full-information benchmark is too clean for the world it is trying to describe.

## The Real Lesson

The real lesson of Coibion and Gorodnichenko 2015 is not just that expectations are sticky.

It is that modern economies depend on belief-updating processes slower, noisier, and more fragile than our clean models admit.

People do not simply receive information and update. They filter. They delay. They discount. They wait for confirmation. They cling to old forecasts because changing your mind too quickly looks reckless, while changing it too slowly looks prudent until the data arrives with a baseball bat.

Information rigidity is not an exotic flaw. It is how institutions behave when attention is scarce and uncertainty is expensive.

And perhaps the darkest implication is that stability itself can manufacture complacency.

A calm economy teaches people not to look too closely. A credible central bank teaches people that inflation is someone else's problem. A long expansion teaches investors that downturns are manageable.

Then the shock arrives. And suddenly everyone updates. Not gradually. Not politely. All at once.

Because the economy does change. People just notice later. And by the time they do, the forecast error has already become history.

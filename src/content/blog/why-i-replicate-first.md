---
title: "Why I replicate papers before extending them"
description: "Replication is unglamorous, which is exactly why it teaches you more than starting from scratch."
date: 2026-07-07
tags: ["research process", "replication"]
---

Every research project I have finished started the same way: take someone else's published result and rebuild it from nothing.

This is unglamorous. Nobody gets excited about reproducing a table that already exists in a journal. But I keep doing it, for three reasons.

First, replication is the fastest way to find out whether you actually understand a paper. Reading a methods section and implementing a methods section are different activities. The paper will leave out a window length, an annualisation convention, or a data filter, and you only discover which details matter when your numbers refuse to match. My drawdown paper hinged on a 22-day window the original never stated explicitly. I only know that because I had to find it.

Second, it gives you a verified foundation. Once your replication matches the published numbers, every extension you build sits on ground you have personally tested. If the extension produces something surprising, you can trust it enough to investigate rather than assume you have a bug.

Third, it builds the right reflex: results are claims, and claims get checked. The same habit that makes you chase a mismatched percentile also makes you run the placebo test that kills your own favourite finding. That has already happened to me once, and the project is better for having died honestly than survived falsely.

Starting from scratch feels more original. But originality built on an unverified base is just risk you have not measured yet.

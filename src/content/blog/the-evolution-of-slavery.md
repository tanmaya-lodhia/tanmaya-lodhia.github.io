Close your eyes for a moment.

Imagine you are part of the ruling elite. Not just rich. Not just influential. Properly powerful. The tiny minority that decides how the world runs.

Now open them.

You have millions of people beneath you, and you need them to wake up every morning and work. Not occasionally. Not when inspiration strikes. Every day, for most of their lives.

How do you make that happen?

For most of history, the answer was brutally simple: force.

You pick up that stone because, if you do not, someone kills you. Crude, direct, effective. Physical slavery was, for centuries, one of the most obvious ways to extract labour from human beings. From ancient empires to the Atlantic slave trade, coercion was the original operating system of work.

But slavery had a serious flaw. It was a terrible management system.

You had to feed enslaved people, house them, guard them, punish them, and deal with the awkward little problem that people who are enslaved do not usually enjoy being enslaved. They do the bare minimum required to avoid punishment. They do not innovate. They do not volunteer for overtime. They do not thank the leadership team for the opportunity.

Worse, coercion is fragile. The moment the guards look away, people run. Or revolt.

So rulers discovered something far more powerful than force.

Belief.

People work harder when they believe their work matters. When they feel their sacrifice has meaning. When they think they are serving something bigger than themselves.

The question changed. It was no longer, "How do we force people to work?" It became, "How do we make people want to?"

## War: Meaning Through Fear

The first great answer was war.

War is excellent at manufacturing purpose. Nothing unites people faster than a shared enemy. It gives life urgency, structure, sacrifice, and moral clarity. Even better, if there is no real enemy available, one can usually be invented.

Wartime economies have often been extraordinarily productive because survival is the strongest motivator there is. People will tolerate rationing, long hours, danger, and exhaustion if they believe the alternative is annihilation.

But war has one inconvenient flaw. It ends.

Eventually, the enemy disappears. Peace breaks out. People start looking around and asking deeply unhelpful questions like, "Why exactly am I still working fourteen hours a day?"

So rulers needed something more permanent. Something people could carry inside their own heads.

## Religion: Labour as Moral Duty

Religion solved the problem beautifully.

What war did temporarily, religion could do permanently. It turned labour into duty. In parts of Christian Europe, work became tied to discipline, virtue, and salvation. Monasteries built farms, roads, libraries, and systems of learning not through chains, but through conviction. Labour was not merely economic. It was spiritual.

This is the kind of thing Max Weber later explored in his idea of the Protestant work ethic: the belief that hard work, discipline, and worldly success could become signs of moral seriousness.

Religion also scaled better than older, localised belief systems. Ancient gods were often attached to specific places, tribes, functions, and rituals. You prayed to one deity for the harvest, another for love, another for war, and all of them seemed capable of ruining your life because someone forgot the correct offering.

Universal religions were a major upgrade. One God. One truth. One moral code. Much cleaner branding.

Christianity and Islam did not merely organise villages or cities. They organised continents. They gave millions of people a shared story, a shared hierarchy, and a shared reason to endure hardship.

But belief fades. Churches split. Science challenges scripture. Secularisation creeps in.

Once again, humanity needed a new operating system.

## The Nation: Belonging as Motivation

Then came the nation.

We are Romans. We are the British Empire. We are the chosen people. We are history's favourite child. We are the pinnacle of civilisation.

And if you work, obey, fight, pay taxes, and occasionally die for the state, you get to be part of something glorious.

This was extremely compelling. Humans love belonging to something bigger than themselves, especially when that something comes with flags, songs, uniforms, monuments, and a socially acceptable excuse to feel superior to foreigners.

The nation transformed labour and sacrifice into patriotism. Work was no longer just about survival. It became part of a civilisational mission.

And, naturally, empires took this logic abroad.

Since we are the greatest civilisation on Earth, it is clearly our duty to sail across the sea and civilise everyone else. We shall teach them our ways, enlighten their souls, take their gold, land, spices, and labour, purely to cover administrative expenses, of course. Then, when it is over, we shall write a beautiful book about how generous we were.

It was a magnificent system. Right up until the conquered people started objecting.

After two world wars, the whole "our civilisation is superior and must dominate the Earth" argument became slightly harder to sell. The imperial myth cracked. National glory, like religion before it, began to lose its grip.

And there was the pattern. Force. War. Religion. Nation. Different systems. Same trick.

Make people believe.

But all of them depended on something external. A master. An enemy. A god. A flag.

So humanity simplified the formula. One symbol. One motivator. One thing people would willingly destroy themselves for.

Money.

## Money: The Perfect Motivator

Money was the perfect invention because it made work feel personal.

Why should I work hard? Because the more I work, the more I get. Not for the king. Not for God. Not for the empire. For me.

That was the genius of it.

Money worked across borders, languages, cultures, and religions. It did not matter who you were. Everyone understood the same magical idea: number goes up, life gets better.

And once that idea took hold, the world went completely mad.

Factories multiplied. Cities exploded. Railways stretched across continents. Production soared. The Industrial Revolution turned humanity from a species that occasionally made chairs into one capable of producing thousands before lunch.

It was one of the greatest productivity booms in history.

But there were three catches.

First, money is all-consuming. Capital wants more capital. If poisoning a river increases profit, congratulations, the river is now poisoned.

Second, money consolidates. Wealth does not politely distribute itself because it has good manners. It piles up. If you already have money, it is easier to make more. Over time, competition becomes monopoly, owners become empires, and rich people become absurdly, almost offensively rich.

Third, money dehumanises. You stop being a person. You become a labour unit, a cost unit, a productivity unit. Your value becomes whatever the market says you are worth this quarter.

Karl Marx looked at this and, in essence, said: this seems bad.

His argument was that capitalism would alienate workers, make them miserable, and eventually push them towards class consciousness and revolt.

And awkwardly enough, he had a point.

Workers did fight back. Not through one grand global revolution, but through strikes, unions, labour parties, protests, and demands for better conditions.

For a brief period in the twentieth century, they even started winning.

## The Post-War Compromise

After the Second World War, much of the industrialised West stumbled into a strange and dangerous idea: what if workers were treated decently?

Wild, I know. Someone should have called security.

From roughly the 1950s to the 1970s, many Western countries built something close to a worker-centred compromise. Wages rose. Unions were stronger. Education became more accessible. Healthcare expanded. Housing was more affordable. A single income could often support a family, a home, a car, and eventually a pension.

The deal was simple: work hard, and you can live a good life.

For once, the motivation problem was being solved with something other than fear, manipulation, or spiritual guilt. Workers received a meaningful share of the gains they helped create.

There was just one problem. The rich hated it.

A worker-centred society does not merely distribute money. It distributes power. Strong unions, public services, and social protections give ordinary people leverage. They make workers harder to exploit and harder to frighten.

And that, naturally, was unacceptable.

So in the 1980s, the rollback began.

In the United States, Reagan attacked organised labour and deregulated markets. In Britain, Thatcher weakened unions, sold public assets, and reshaped the economy around market logic. Across much of the West, the post-war compromise was dismantled piece by piece.

But this created a new problem.

You cannot simply make life worse for millions of people and expect them to smile politely through it. People might revolt. Very inconvenient. Terrible for quarterly earnings.

So the system needed a replacement. Something that felt empowering while keeping people obedient.

That replacement was consumerism.

## Consumerism: The Final Upgrade

The promise changed.

Before, it was: work hard, and you can build a good life.

Now it became: work hard, and you can buy more stuff.

Identity moved from character to consumption. You were no longer defined primarily by your work, family, community, or values. You were defined by your purchases.

The car. The phone. The watch. The house. The holiday. The brand. The lifestyle.

"I am a hardworking father, a loyal friend, and an honest member of my community."

Cute. But what colour is your Bugatti?

Consumerism solved a problem that every previous system struggled with. It made people compete with one another instead of organising together.

Give five hundred people a million pounds each. What happens? They all buy houses. Then they post the houses online. Then everyone sees everyone else's house. Then nobody feels special anymore. So they want a bigger house.

Then they borrow, spend, compete, compare, and slowly bankrupt themselves emotionally, financially, and socially.

Consumerism turns potential collaborators into rivals. It atomises society. It makes solidarity difficult, not through violence, but through desire.

You cannot organise a revolution when everyone is too busy trying to upgrade.

And that is the real genius of the system.

A traditional slave knows they are enslaved. That knowledge is dangerous, because it creates the possibility of rebellion.

A consumer does not know. Worse, the consumer thinks they chose it.

No one forces you to buy the newer phone, the bigger car, the more impressive flat, the luxury holiday you cannot quite afford. No one puts a sword to your throat. You participate voluntarily. You enjoy it. You get little hits of pleasure from the very system that keeps you exhausted.

That is what makes it so effective.

The chains are invisible. Self-imposed. Comfortable. And available on monthly finance.
